# Stanford d.school Design Thinking Framework

## Overview

Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation. It draws from the designer's toolkit to integrate the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success.

## The Five Phases

### 1. Empathize
**Purpose:** Understand the people you're designing for.

Work to understand the user experience through observation, interaction, and immersion. Set aside your own assumptions. Build empathy through:
- **Observe** — watch users in their natural context
- **Engage** — interview and talk with users
- **Immerse** — experience what users experience

Key outputs: interview notes, observation notes, empathy maps.

### 2. Define
**Purpose:** Frame the right problem to solve.

Synthesize empathy findings into a clear problem statement. Use the POV (Point of View) framework:

> **[User]** needs **[need]** because **[insight]**

Good POV statements are:
- Focused on specific users (not "everyone")
- Based on insights (not assumptions)
- Generative (inspire many possible solutions)
- Not too broad (can't solve everything) or too narrow (limits creativity)

"How Might We" (HMW) questions reframe the POV into actionable creative prompts:
- Too broad: "How might we improve education?" — Useless
- Too narrow: "How might we add a search bar?" — Predetermined
- Just right: "How might we help students find relevant resources when they're stuck?" — Generative

### 3. Ideate
**Purpose:** Generate a wide range of possible solutions.

Rules for ideation:
- **Defer judgment** — no idea is bad during brainstorming
- **Go for quantity** — more ideas = more chances for breakthrough
- **Build on others' ideas** — "yes, and..." not "yes, but..."
- **Be visual** — sketch, diagram, prototype in words
- **One conversation at a time** — let each idea breathe
- **Stay on topic** — wild is good, irrelevant is not
- **Encourage wild ideas** — the most impractical idea often contains the seed of the best solution

### 4. Prototype
**Purpose:** Build to think, not to ship.

Prototypes are:
- **Quick** — hours, not weeks
- **Cheap** — minimal investment
- **Disposable** — you should be willing to throw them away
- **Specific** — each prototype tests a specific assumption
- **Tangible** — something people can interact with

The goal is to learn, not to prove you're right. A prototype that reveals a fatal flaw is more valuable than one that confirms what you already believed.

### 5. Test
**Purpose:** Get feedback and refine.

Testing is NOT validation — it's learning:
- **Show, don't tell** — let users experience the prototype without explanation
- **Create experiences** — set up scenarios, not presentations
- **Observe** — watch what they do, not just what they say
- **Iterate** — testing always reveals something to improve

## Key Principles

- **Human-centered** — start with people, not technology
- **Bias toward action** — prototyping over planning
- **Radical collaboration** — diverse perspectives improve outcomes
- **Culture of prototyping** — fail early, fail cheap
- **Show don't tell** — communicate through tangible artifacts
- **Mindful of process** — know where you are and why

## Non-Linear Process

The phases are not strictly sequential. You may:
- Return to Empathize after Testing reveals misunderstood needs
- Jump from Define back to Empathize if the problem is mis-framed
- Iterate between Ideate and Prototype rapidly
- Run multiple cycles through the full process

The framework is a guide, not a prison.
